A/B Test & Hypothesis
Prioritization — E-commerce

Applied ICE and RICE frameworks to rank revenue-growth hypotheses, then designed and analyzed a full A/B experiment to determine which user group drove better conversion and order performance.

01 — Problem

Context & Objective

An e-commerce team had a backlog of potential improvements — new features, UX changes, and promotional strategies — each claiming to drive revenue growth. The challenge was deciding which ones to actually test, and in what order. After prioritization, a live A/B experiment was run to validate the impact of the highest-priority change on real users.

The project is structured in two distinct parts: first, a framework-driven prioritization exercise to identify where to focus testing efforts; second, a rigorous statistical analysis of the A/B experiment's results across conversion rate and average order size.

02 — Hypothesis Prioritization

ICE vs RICE Scoring

Two scoring frameworks were applied to a set of potential hypotheses, each offering a different perspective on prioritization:

ICE (Impact · Confidence · Ease) — A simpler model that scores purely on expected outcome, team confidence, and implementation effort. It surfaces quick wins and high-confidence bets, but ignores how many users will actually be affected.

RICE (Reach · Impact · Confidence · Effort) — Adds a Reach dimension, weighting each hypothesis by the number of users it would affect. This shifts the ranking toward changes that benefit larger segments, often revealing that high-confidence "quick wins" only impact a small portion of the user base.

Under ICE, hypotheses 8, 0, and 7 ranked highest. RICE reordered the list considerably, reflecting that some top ICE hypotheses would reach far fewer users than alternatives — making them less impactful at scale. The divergence between frameworks informed which hypothesis to prioritize for live testing.

03 — Experiment Results

Cumulative Revenue & Order Patterns

With the highest-priority hypothesis selected, two user groups (A and B) were assigned different experiences and monitored throughout August. Several dimensions were tracked to build a complete picture of behavioral differences.

04 — Outlier Detection & Significance

Anomaly Filtering & Statistical Tests

Before running significance tests, the data was explored for outliers at the order level to avoid skewed results from anomalous users placing unusually high numbers of orders or paying extreme prices.

Fig. 5 — Orders per user scatter. The vast majority cluster at 1 order per conversion. Outliers (2–4+ orders) exist but are rare, and extreme cases (4+ orders) are flagged as anomalous.
Fig. 6 — Order price distribution. Most orders fall between 200–400 units. High-price outliers above the 95th and 99th percentile thresholds were identified and handled during the filtering step.

Statistical significance was tested on both raw and filtered datasets using z-tests, testing for differences in (a) conversion rate and (b) average order size between groups.

05 — Conclusion

Decision & Takeaways

The analysis clearly establishes that Group B outperformed Group A in conversion rate — a finding that held consistent across both raw and filtered datasets. This result is statistically significant and can be attributed to the tested change, not random variation.

However, the average order size did not differ significantly between the two groups. This is a meaningful insight: the improvement driven by Group B's experience is in bringing more users to convert, not in increasing how much each converting user spends. Revenue growth, in this case, is a volume play — not a value-per-transaction play.

Business recommendation: Stop the test and roll out Group B's experience to all users. The conversion lift is real and statistically validated. Future experimentation should focus on increasing average order value — the dimension where neither group currently has an edge — through strategies like upselling, bundling, or minimum-order thresholds.
